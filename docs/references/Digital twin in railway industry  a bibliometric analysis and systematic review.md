REVIEW ARTICLE Digital twin in railway industry: a bibliometric analysis and systematic review Zi-Yang Zhang, Du Shang and Shuai Su School of Automation and Intelligence, Beijing Jiaotong University, Beijing, China ABSTRACT In light of the ongoing advancements in information technology, the railway industry is consistently striving to enhance its digitalisation and intelligence. Furthermore, digital twin, which enables the integration between the physical and digital world, has also garnered attention within the railway industry in recent years. In order to gain a comprehensive understanding of the current status of the application of digital twin in the railway industry, this paper offers an in-depth analysis of 177 pieces of related literature. A bibliometric analysis was conducted on the 177 related literatures that were collected, which included publication trend analysis and keyword co- occurrence analysis. A bibliometric analysis was conducted to ascertain the compos- ition of the four layers of railway digital twin, which are subsequently introduced in detail: data, model, application, and framework. In light of literature analysis, this study summarises the challenges encountered in applying digital twin in the railway industry and suggests possible future research directions. This chapter aims to introduce the current state and research trajectory in railway digital twin technology, based on the bibliometric analysis results of relevant research content. 4.1. Data Data is the core driver of the railway digital twin. The utilisation of multi-dimensional data collection, real-time data communication, data fusion, and analysis facilitates the implementation of railway digital twin, thereby enabling the realisation of various applications. These applications include, but are not limited to, the management of railway assets, the construction of geometric models, fault diagnosis, and predictive maintenance [69]. Consequently, the primary objective of this section is to introduce and analyse the data collection, management, and other aspects of research in railway digital twin. The keyword co-occurrence network diagram reveals a close relationship between BIM and the information of railway digital twin.BIM is a parametric 3D geometric building model that contains detailed information and data, and is suitable for full - life - cycle management of facilities and equip- ment [202]. In the digital twin for life cycle management of linear infrastructure in railway, researchers have indicated that, due to the data sharing, management, and exchange features of BIM, it is capable of satisfying the needs for multi-party collaboration, structured management, and three-dimensional creation in the digital twin for linear infrastructure [54]. Additionally, the integration of field data information across multiple dimensions is facilitated by BIM. For instance, a 6D BIM is proposed in the digital twin-assisted life cycle management of railway turnout systems. This approach utilises Revit 2018 to establish a 3D model based on the 2D drawings of the railway turnout. Subsequently, the turn- out project time schedule information is added to the 3D model to form a 4D BIM. Then, the cost data of the turnout project is integrated to form a 5D BIM. Finally, carbon emission data is added to form a 6D BIM on the basis of a 5D BIM. The 6D BIM can provide three-dimensional geometric infor- mation, cost, time, and carbon emission data, as well as other multi-dimensional information, thereby offering an information basis for the turnout full life cycle management [117]. In addition to the multi- dimensional information that can be provided using 6D BIM in turnout systems, BIM can also provide multi-dimensional information in railway bridge infrastructure [182]. In the railway digital twin previously referenced, the BIM predominantly offers geometric parameter data derived from static design models, such as two-dimensional designs of turnouts. This information is devoid of real-time characteristics. The advent of technological advances has prompted researchers to adopt novel methodologies for data collection, encompassing the utilisation of lidar, the Internet of Things (IoT), and cutting-edge sensor technologies. In order to address the dearth of data resulting from the limited utilisation of computer aided design (CAD) during the nascent stages of railway con- struction, researchers employed point cloud data collected by airborne light detection and ranging (LIDAR) to ascertain the geometric parameters of railway masts [55]. There are also studies that use airborne laser scanning (ALS) to collect point cloud data to obtain structural parameters of railway tracks [34]. In order to satisfy the real-time data transmission requirements of railway digital twin, Supervisory control and data acquisition (SCADA) systems can be utilised to obtain data information on railway equipment and components, such as signals and turnouts [121]. Some studies have been conducted that employ the IoT for the purpose of intelligent data collection. One such example is the use of IoT in vehicle equipment, such as pantographs and bogies. This particular IoT employs sensors (e.g. accelerometers and inclinometers) and communication from vehicle equipment IoT to provide real-time status information regarding vehicle equipment for vehicle digital twin [115]. This, in turn, establishes a data foundation for creating vehicle digital twin. In addition to using traditional sensors such as strain sensors, acceleration sensors, and displacement sensors for data collection in railway digital twin [167], some new types of sensors have also been applied. For example, triboelectric nano- generators can be used for monitoring vibration, rail fastener tightness, and other parameters, and can also achieve self-powering [189]. DIGITAL TWIN 9

The railway industry is a complex system engineering project that encompasses multiple aspects, including infrastructure construction, vehicle manufacturing and maintenance, and operational manage- ment. Consequently, the implementation of a railway digital twin system necessitates the storage of sub- stantial data, including location data, track geometry data, speed data, vibration data, temperature data, and log data [128]. Concurrently, this data may encompass multiple business units, thereby creating data silos. The existence of data silos has been shown to reduce data availability, affect the efficiency of collaboration between different departments, and also lead to waste of data resources and increased data collection costs [128]. A proposed solution to the challenges posed by heterogeneous data sources and the issue of data silos is a digital twin architecture. This architecture includes data sources, a wrap- per layer, a digital twin aggregation hierarchy, a services network, and management services. This archi- tecture was preliminarily evaluated in the context of railway infrastructure maintenance management [76]. In summary, the initial stage of data collection in railway digital twin utilises BIM, which has since evolved to incorporate laser scanning, the IoT, and various new sensors to acquire railway data. Given the complexity of the railway industry and its cross-disciplinary, cross-departmental nature, the data involved is vast and contains a significant amount of heterogeneous data. Addressing issues related to heterogeneous data and data silos is essential for the successful implementation of railway digital twin. 4.2. Model The establishment of models serves as the foundational element for digital twin to map physical entities and provide functional services and application implementations [203]. The keyword timeline graph reveals that, in the nascent stages of railway digital twin research, the primary focus was on the study of geometric digital twin, that is, the establishment of geometric models. For instance, in 2019, researchers employed Revit and two-dimensional drawings of turnouts to create an accurate three- dimensional model. This 3D model contained detailed and precise geometric information, such as lay- out, contours, and spatial relationships between components [117]. In addition to employing design drawings to generate geometric models, research is being conducted on the use of point cloud data for this purpose. For instance, in the development of geometric models for railway masts, principal compo- nent analysis is employed for the preprocessing of point cloud data. This is followed by the removal of noise through the implementation of threshold distance settings and the elimination of ground point cloud data to minimise computational demands. Subsequently, a random sample consistency algorithm is implemented for detection, and two refinement algorithms are employed to distinguish railway masts from other pole-shaped objects. Finally, the point cloud data is utilised to establish a 3D model in industry foundation classes format [55]. Similar methods have also been applied to the creation of geo- metric models for railway tracks [34]. From the keyword timeline grap1, it can be seen that after the focus on geometric models, railway digital twin gradually shifted to research on damage detection and predictive maintenance. During this period, two predominant model establishment methods emerged: one based on physical mechanism models and the other on data-driven models. A physical mechanism model is a mathematical model established based on the operational mecha- nisms and physical principles of railway facilities and equipment. For instance, a study developed a digital twin model of a lithium-ion battery based on its 3D microstructure for railway vehicles. The model employed finite element methods to simulate thermal behaviour and calculate electrochemical and current distribution. This was done to reflect the internal structure of the battery and the heat gen- eration of its components [136]. In order to analyse the thermal behaviour of traction motors, a study utilised a combination of reduced-order modelling, thermal calculation, and fluid mechanics to simulate fluid flow and thermal effects within and outside the motor. Thermal calculations and fluid mechanics were employed to simulate fluid flow and thermal effects within and around the motor, thereby analy- sing heat generation in critical motor components [142]. Another study developed a digital twin model based on ‘Peridynamics’ (a non-local continuum mechanics theory), combined with new fatigue dam- age assessment methods and adaptive time mapping techniques, to simulate and analyse fatigue cracks in railway turnout points from a fracture mechanics perspective [90]. Data-driven models are established through the processing and analysis of substantial observational data sets, independent of physical laws or the operational mechanisms of facilities and equipment. In the context of railway digital twin research, the development of data-driven models frequently relies on the utilisation of artificial intelligence methodologies. To illustrate, model-based reinforcement learning methods are employed to achieve the control of virtual coupled train sets [65]. The method’s primary function is to utilise a deep neural network to calibrate the train dynamics model. The model’s inputs encompass the train’s speed, position, and acceleration, while its outputs correspond to alterations in the train’s state. Model-based reinforcement learning employs the output of the deep neural network to generate and optimise control strategies [65]. Another study utilises a reinforcement learning model based on the Advantage actor critic algorithm to enable the agent to select appropriate maintenance times and behaviours based on track conditions, thereby minimising maintenance costs and defect occurrence rates [179]. The model’s state encompasses track geometric parameters and component defects. The action space comprises a range of maintenance behaviours. The reward function is defined as maintenance costs and defect occurrence rates. These three conditions are used to establish the reinforcement learning model [179]. In order to satisfy the requirements for defect detection in railway component digital twin, a study has been conducted that established a data-driven model using a dynamically weighted convolutional neural network (CNN) for defect detection [93]. This model trains each CNN model using images of railway components as input and dynamically adjusts the weights of each CNN model’s output to obtain the final prediction results [93]. In addition to the aforementioned deep learning and reinforcement learning methods, research on railway digital twin has also focused on the establishment of data-driven models for 3D data. For instance, a study employed the geometric parameters of tracks as 3D stacked inputs into a three-dimensional recurrent neural network to achieve track geometric shape prediction [188]. A study was conducted to address the segmentation of large amounts of point cloud data. The study proposed a point cloud semantic segmentation method called 3DGraphseg. This study proposes a local embedding super-point graph to simplify point cloud data, and then uses a 3D deep learning-based gated integrated graph convolutional network to achieve point cloud semantic segmentation in high-speed railway environments [21]. In railway digital twin research, in addition to using physical mechanism models and data-driven models separately, there are also studies that combine the two models to achieve better results. For instance, to address issues such as cutting errors, inadequate splicing accuracy, and steel deformation in the construction control of large-span spatial steel structures, a study proposed a method using a finite element model to predict temperature changes during the construction process of spatial steel struc- tures via a long short-term memory network (LSTM). This method has been employed in the construc- tion of a high-speed railway station [89]. In the track stiffness testing, to address the issue of missing axle box data, a study has employed finite element models to emulate the mechanical interrelationships among rails, rail pads, sleepers, ballast, and ancillary components. These models have facilitated the establishment of a correlation between track behaviour and axle box acceleration, thereby constructing a physical mechanism model. The simulation data generated by the physical mechanism model is then employed as training data for a gaussian process regression model [87]. This approach capitalises on the precision of the physical mechanism model and the effectiveness of data-driven models. Another study established a vehicle-track coupled dynamics model based on physical laws and finite element models. Then, Bayesian inference was utilised in the data-driven model component to update the parameters of the physical mechanism model. Gaussian process regression was also employed to predict errors, thereby integrating the physical mechanism model with the data-driven model [123]. A comprehensive review of the extant research on railway digital twin models reveals a predominant focus on three primary categories: geometric models, physical mechanism models, and data-driven models. As railway digital twin have evolved, the establishment of models has transitioned from early single-device and single-scenario models to more complex device and scenario models. Framework In the context of railway digital twin, the overarching objective of the digital twin framework is to facilitate the integration of models, data, and applications, thereby enabling effective resource allocation and coordination. An analysis of extant research on railway digital twin reveals that the majority of frameworks are customised for specific scenarios. For instance, a study proposed a framework for smart railway passenger stations, with the objective of enhancing operational efficiency and service quality. This framework is based on digital twin and encompasses the physical layer, I/O layer, data layer, model layer, application layer, and human-machine interface layer [96]. The physical layer encompasses stations, personnel, vehicles, and buildings. The I/O layer comprises sensing devices and actuators, which establish a connection between the physical layer and the data layer. The data layer stores static, dynamic, and interactive data. The model layer constitutes a digital twin model of the station, con- structed using data. The application layer incorporates application services such as resource manage- ment, energy management, and business management. The human-machine interface layer provides feedback on information [96]. In the context of digital twin for trains, current vehicle dynamics simula- tion software has been identified as deficient in meeting the demands of digital twin train development. These demands include real-time performance, model complexity, and resource management. To address these challenges, a research has proposed a cloud-based railway vehicle dynamics simulation software framework. This framework encompasses parallel task flows, simulation resource sharing, and hybrid 3D visualisation methods [184]. In the domain of railway bridge inspection and maintenance, a research has proposed a framework for a bridge digital twin, encompassing physical entities, virtual entities, services, and connections. In addition to proposing railway digital twin frameworks for specific applications, some studies have also proposed general-purpose frameworks [66]. For instance, a study has been proposed that involves a lifetime learning-enabled modelling framework for digital twin, which encompasses supervised learning modelling, cross-domain model adaptation, and lifetime living models. This framework was employed to vehicle fault diagnosis, thereby demonstrating the feasibility of migrating digital twin models [126]. Another study proposes a digital twin framework for railway systems, which includes data sensing, collection, and storage; data preprocessing and artificial intelli- gence inference. This framework is applied to defect identification of railway wheels and railway turn- outs [155]. A survey of railway digital twin frameworks reveals a tendency to prioritise railway health monitor- ing and fault diagnosis. However, notable variations emerge among frameworks designed for distinct application scenarios, potentially impacting scalability and coordination across systems and devices. 5. In the context of railway digital twin, models are predicated on data, applications are contingent on models, and frameworks facilitate the integration of these elements. The integration of these four layers is pivotal in the development of railway digital twin. Challenges Challenges in the data: Despite the continuous evolution of novel methodologies for data collection in railway digital twin, persistent challenges and issues persist. Firstly, due to the nascent state of technol- ogy and the absence of digital records of historical lines during the early stages of railway construction, there is a paucity of railway data [176]. In order to compensate for the dearth of data in the establish- ment of railway digital twin, it is imperative to collect railway data. However, it is imperative to con- sider the inherent characteristics of railways during the process of data collection. For instance, when collecting data on railway infrastructure and its surrounding environment, it is necessary to consider the complex factors of the railway’s surrounding environment, such as weather, lighting, visibility, and obstructions caused by intrusive objects, which can affect image data and point cloud data [55]. Furthermore, due to the intricate nature of the railway system, which encompasses a multitude of facili- ties and equipment, the strategic placement and optimisation of sensor locations must be taken into account during data collection. The proliferation of sensors can lead to an increase in data volume and costs, consequently reducing the efficiency of digital twin [85]. With respect to multi-source heteroge- neous data, the railway industry has long confronted challenges related to data fragmentation and data silos across design, construction, and operations phases. For instance, BIM technology is employed in the domains of railway design and construction; however, a lack of integration with operational data persists [188]. Conversely, railway data is derived from a variety of sources, including IoT, LIDAR point clouds, and video images. Furthermore, the railway industry comprises numerous departments and disciplines, which complicates data integration. Secondly, given the extensive nature of railway lines, which often span hundreds or even thousands of kilometres and contain a multitude of devices and components, the data to be collected is extensive, high-density, and voluminous [21]. The manage- ment of voluminous data, encompassing data collection, organisation, and processing, poses a substan- tial challenge for railway digital twin. Challenges in the model: The construction of railway digital twin models is characterised by a multi- tude of complexities, which can be categorised into three distinct dimensions: data, environment, and mechanisms. For instance, the absence of comprehensive data hinders the development of high- precision railway infrastructure geometric models. Despite the proposal of certain studies to utilise the standardised design and structural repetitiveness of railways to circumvent data shortages and repetitive work, thereby achieving automated modelling, the high safety and reliability requirements of railways continue to pose challenges to such automation [55]. The intricacy of railway environments and mech- anisms contributes to the complexity of developing railway digital twin models. The wheel-rail contact relationship, for instance, is influenced by numerous factors, including wear, deformation, thermody- namics, and vibration [123]. In the event that vehicle, track, and other subsystems are analysed in isola- tion, model separation and loss of accuracy will result. The establishment of a high-fidelity digital twin model within the context of the multidimensional complexity inherent to railways poses a significant challenge in the development of railway digital twin models. The interpretability of the model repre- sents a significant challenge in the development of railway digital twin. Despite the utilisation of artifi- cial intelligence methodologies, including deep learning and reinforcement learning, in the development of models for railway defect detection, train control, and other applications, these methods remain black-box models and are inadequate in meeting the railway industry’s stringent requirements for safety and reliability. Consequently, the interpretability of certain AI methodologies is a hindrance to their implementation in the development of railway digital twin models. Furthermore, high-fidelity models have the capacity to satisfy the stringent simulation requirements associated with high precision, includ- ing real-time simulation of train dynamics [184]. However, high-fidelity models have high demands on computational and hardware resources. Consequently, balancing model accuracy with computational costs is another major challenge for railway digital twin. Conventional mainten- ance methodologies are predicated on predetermined schedules and the expertise of human operators, a paradigm that engenders elevated maintenance expenses and the potential for suboptimal maintenance practices. Although a number of studies have explored the use of reinforcement learning-based digital twin for the purpose of planning maintenance schedules [179], the application of these digital twin remains limited to individual pieces of equipment. Given the complexity of railway systems and the wide range of equipment they manage, proposing a separate digital twin framework for each piece of equipment and application scenario could lead to insufficient coordination between systems. Despite the proposal of gen- eric frameworks by certain studies, their primary focus has been on the inspection of railway health status [155]. Implementation in other application scenarios, such as vehicle control or scheduling, has not been documented. Consequently, railway digital twin frameworks encounter challenges associated with the absence of generic and standardised frameworks. Furthermore, there is a paucity of research on evaluation methods and frameworks for railway digital twin platforms. Future After conducting a literature review on railway digital twin, this paper proposes the following research directions that may require attention in the future development of railway digital twin. In terms of data:  Research sensors suitable for complex railway environments (such as tunnels and high altitudes) for railway data collection and to solve problems such as noise interference.  How to process and analyse data affected by interference.  For railway facilities with insufficient data, how to use generative adversarial networks and other generative methods to generate data, providing a data foundation for railway digital twin.  Establish a platform for managing and analysing multi-source heterogeneous data to avoid data silos and achieve the integration of railway infrastructure and equipment data throughout their entire lifecycle. In terms of model:  Transitioning from the establishment of railway digital twin models for single devices or single application scenarios to model combinations and model fusion [60,178].  Given the high requirements for safety and reliability in railways, the fusion of physical mechanism models and data-driven models is utilised, with the addition of physical constraints to enhance the interpretability of the models.  Develop railway digital twin models of varying precision to address different application require- ments. For example, high-fidelity digital twin models can be established for high-precision simula- tion of train dynamics models. If the goal is merely to validate the feasibility of railway facility and equipment design schemes, low-fidelity models can be established to facilitate rapid iteration and design validation. Conclusion The railway industry has been engaged in a sustained effort to embrace digitalisation and intelligence in recent years. The concept of digital twin has garnered significant attention in academic and indus- trial circles, suggesting a promising avenue for future exploration. In this paper, 177 literature sources pertaining to the application of digital twin in the railway industry are analysed and systematically reviewed using Cite Space and Vos Viewer. The analysis of these 177 literatures reveals a notable growth trend in the application of digital twin technology in the railway industry in recent years. This paper introduces railway digital twin based on the four layers of data, models, applications, and frameworks derived from Bibliometric Result Analysis. The integration of bibliometric result analysis with a comprehensive literature review facilitates the identification of the challenges encountered by railway digital twin. These challenges encompass data collection, the processing of multi-source hetero- geneous data, the management of multi-dimensional data complexity, the multi-dimensional complexity of data, environment, and mechanisms in model establishment, the limitation of application scale, and the absence of standardised frameworks. Furthermore, this paper offers a synopsis of prospective research directions for future consideration, including the development of sensors adapted to the intri- cate railway environment, the establishment of data platforms, and the integration of physical mechan- ism models with data-driven models.
